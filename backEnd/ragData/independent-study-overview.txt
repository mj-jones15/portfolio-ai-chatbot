My independent study project focused on analyzing how Responsible AI (RAI) research influences national and international AI governance frameworks. The project combined natural language processing, embedding-based semantic analysis, and large-scale citation mapping to evaluate the relationship between academic RAI literature and real-world policy documents.

To build the dataset, I collected papers from major AI ethics conferences including NeurIPS and AIES, alongside national and international AI governance documents from organizations such as UNESCO, the United States, China, and Egypt. Using the Semantic Scholar API, I constructed a pipeline that extracted metadata, abstracts, citation relationships, and keyword distributions from hundreds of research papers. I then generated semantic embeddings using the e5-large-v2 embedding model and compared policy documents against academic literature using cosine similarity scoring and citation overlap analysis.

One of the project’s major findings was that Egypt and UNESCO demonstrated the strongest research grounding, with Egypt leading the dataset at 48 highly aligned papers connected to its governance framework. In contrast, China showed the highest density of Responsible AI terminology in policy language, but had zero papers surpassing the research consensus threshold, representing the largest gap between topical ambition and measurable academic grounding. U.S. policy documents clustered in the middle of the dataset, showing moderate alignment with the broader research landscape while still leaving room for stronger academic integration.

The project also revealed major thematic disconnects between research and policy. Fairness overwhelmingly dominated academic RAI paper production, explainability received the strongest citation influence across the literature, while accountability emerged as the dominant concept in policy language itself. Sustainability, despite appearing in many governance frameworks, was nearly absent from both research production and citation impact, suggesting a significant blind spot in the current Responsible AI ecosystem.

Overall, the project explored not only which Responsible AI ideas are being researched, but which ideas ultimately shape governance and public policy at a global scale.
